Bristol Samba Notes

This page contains Tom's notes about Samba drumming. It can also play the patterns as a reminder. The drums are synthesised rather than recorded, so they sound a bit off. The low-frequency component of the surdos gets lost without good speakers or headphones.

Instruments

In Samba we have seen seven instruments so far:
The caixa / snare
A high-pitched two skinned drum with rattles that add some noise .
The repinique
A slightly lower pitched two skinned drum played with nylon beaters which makes a ringing sound .
The 1st and 2nd Surdo
Two large bass drums which are sometimes called fundos because they go at the back. The 1st has the lowest note , and the second a bit higher .
The 3rd and 4th Surdo
Two smaller bass drums, again in increasing order of pitch. The 3rds often seem to play rolls, and the 4ths patterns with off-beats in.
Agogo bells
These are handbells played with a stick. One bell is tuned a bit higher than the other. Here I've said that the high bell is on the accents and the low bell the normal notes.

Patterns & Breaks

The music we have played so far is made of patterns of rhythm, grouped into families like Samba Reggae. Within a family each drum has its own set of patterns which are given numbers. The leader indicates which pattern to play by making a circle with their thumb and forefinger (üëå), and then showing a number of fingers (üñê).

Within the pattern, time is divided into beats. Most of the patterns we've learned so far are 1,2,4 or 8 beats long, after which they repeat themselves. When keeping a constant tempo all the beats are the same length in time. A faster tempo means shorter beats (so you can fit more into the same time).

Each beat is chopped up into equal intervals where notes lie. Often there are four of these, called sixteenths, but in some patterns we also play triplets where a note is a third of a beat.

Some of the notes in a beat have a name - the first sixteenth is the on-beat, and the third is the off-beat. Maybe the others have names too?

Breaks

Each family also has a set of breaks. These all seem to work a bit differently. The leader indicates a break by making a capital T-shape with their forearms and then a number with their fingers (except for one break which just seems to be sticks-held-sideways).

Some breaks are a call-and-response between the leader (or some of the drums) and other drums. Others are like a normal pattern, which plays in a loop.

When going into a break, it seems like we usually play a final beat number one before starting the break.

When leaving a break, it seems like the break often includes a beat one which is absorbed by the pattern we are returning to.
